State Spending on Community Living
for People with Disabilities

Published by ADA-PARC — September 2025

ada

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) mandates the right to live in the community with supports instead of living in a nursing home or long-term care institution. This right was upheld by the U.S. Supreme Court in the landmark Olmstead decision in 1999.1

Despite this right to live in the community, some states are doing worse on overall community spending, and some are doing better to support people with disabilities to live in the community. The map and tables below display total state spending per Medicaid recipient, inclusive of all waiver types (Community First Choice, Section 1915(i), Section 1915(c), Section 1515), for people with disabilities (all ages) living in the community.2

Lower spending means fewer financial resources for people with disabilities to live in the community — or more spent on institutions like nursing homes. Higher spending means better financial support for community living. Rebalancing money toward community living remains an ADA-related disparity issue for many people with disabilities, putting them at higher risk for institutionalization. Currently, this community living support is highly dependent on the zip code and state in which you live.

State Map

Note on map color direction: For this indicator, lighter colors indicate more spending (better performance), and darker colors indicate less spending (worse performance). This is the reverse of typical maps where darker = more.
